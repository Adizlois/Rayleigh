2. Landsat Satellites

Landsat satellites have been providing multispectral images of the Earth continuously since the early 1970's. Hence, a unique 44 year data record of the Earth's land surface is now available. This portrait of the Earth's surface has been (and still is) used across many different disciplines to achieve improved understanding of the Earth's land surfaces and the impact of humans on the environment.
Landsat revisits the same scene every 16 days, resulting in a change of acquisition geometry that will impact surface reflectance. Scenes cover approximately a region of 180x180 km, and data can be acquired over seven bands within the optical domain.

Landsat images were downloaded from the USGS Landsat archive as Level 1T products. Landsat Ecosystem Disturbance Adaptive Processing System (LEDAPS) data is also available on USGS website for the study area. Other sensors of interest for studying water quality are SPOT, Quickbird, etc. MERIS and MODIS have also been employed, but their coarse resolution limit their usefulness to the larger water bodies.
