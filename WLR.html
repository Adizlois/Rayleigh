4. Water leaving reflectances retrieval

At-sensor radiance Lt(λ) consists of contributions from Rayleigh scattering Lray(λ), aerosol scattering La(λ), and water-leaving radiance Lw(λ) (Zhang et al, 1999):


(2) L t ( λ ) = L ray ( λ ) + L a ( λ ) + τ( λ ) L w ( λ )
where τ(λ) is the air-path attenuation coefficient or diffuse transmittance coefficient. In here, we ignore any Rayleigh-aerosol multiple scattering, and if the solar zenith angle is not very small, dazzle can be ignored (Chen, 1998). Lt(λ) is obtained from the calibrated Landsat TM data, and Lray(λ) can be calculated precisely. So the key unknown parameter would be La(λ) in order to estimate water leaving radiances Lw(λ) for each band.

4.2 Estimation of the aerosol contribution (La)

The aerosol contribution can be estimated by using the clear-water-pixels approach from the blue, green, and red TM bands of TM (Ouaidrari and Vermote, 1999). This approach locates pixels with a known radiance (e.g. dense dark vegetation for the land surface, or in the case of water, clear waters with a typical response), and estimates the aerosol contribution from the difference between measured radiance and this known target radiance. 4.3 Clear-Water Pixels Identification

The method introduced above requires the identification of pixels in the water body where the water is clear because in such cases we can estimate the aerosol contribution from this pixels and interpolate our results to other nearby pixels. Thereby, the method used to identify clear water pixels can meaningfully affect the obtained results. Several approaches have been developed with the objective of analysing this variability.
4.3.1 SWIR threshold
At first and similar to Wang's, mid-infrared band was analyzed to identify clear-water pixels. The basis of this method is that aerosol fraction in this band, for clear water, is supossed to be negligible. And therefore, once rayleigh fraction has been computed (eq.3), we can easily calculate water leaving radiance:
As said, aerosol scattering was ignored in this band, and water-leaving radiance at mid-infrared band Lw (2.1μm) was computed following Equation (14) and Equation (15):

(15) t(2.1μm)Lw (2.1 μm) = Lt (2.1 μm) - Lray (2.1μm)

Water leaving radiance (Lw(2.1μm)) was then used to identify clear water pixels, general water pixels and non-water pixels. If Lw(2.1μm)<0.2, this pixel is classified to clear water dataset; 0.2≤Lw(2.1μm)<0.9, general water dataset; Lw(2.1μm)≥ 0.9, non-water dataset.
4.3.2 Multivariate Distribution And Maximum Likelihood
Perhaps a more robust way of selecting clear water pixels is to use classification techniques. This allows the probabilistic selection of a set of pixels that have a similar spectral signature to clear water. The simplest form of the classification is maximum likelihood, in which we assume that the spectral response over different TM bands can be modelled as a multivariate distribution (and hence, by a mean reflectance vector and a covariance matrix). The mean vector and covariance matrix are obtained from a set of reference spectra. A new, unlabeled spectrum is given a probability of belonging to the clear water class by calculating its probability conditioned by the previoulsy chosen means and covariance estimates.
Irrespective of the method chosen, the essential practical steps usually include:
  1. Deciding on the set classes into which to segment the image.
  2. Choosing known, representative pixels for each of the classes.
  3. Using the training data to estimate the parameters of the particular classifier algorithm to be employed.
  4. Using the trained classifier to label every pixel in the image as belonging to one of the classes specified in step 1. Here the whole image is classified.
In this specific case, MLC was used to assign each pixel to one specific spectral class (clear water pixels) at a 95 % confidence level.

Despite of the fact that identification process could be performed with every single available band, only spectral signature including NIR (4) and SWIR(7) bands was taken into account in this study as those bands are the ones that can better describe surface conditions of water reservoirs (showing whether water is turbid or clean, shallow or deep and more). Mid-infrared band is less sensitive to aerosol scattering compared with visible bands, but it is still sensitive to the surface characteristics (Kaufman and Sendra, 1988) and near infrared band can help us to identify shallow waters as well as turbid areas more precisely.
Having established the clear-water pixel distribution, defined by its mean vector and covariance matrix, pixels matching can be easily performed by applying a chi-square test (assuming |Σ|≠0):
